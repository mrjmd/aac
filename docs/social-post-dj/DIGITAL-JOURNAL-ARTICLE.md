## Draft Article

### The New England Foundation Repair Company That Built a Business by Talking People Out of Repairs

*(Image here)*

*Photo Courtesy of:*

"Hey Matt, I think my foundation is failing. There's a crack running across the wall."

"Send me a picture."

A few seconds pass.

"Okay — I see it. That's not structural. It looks scary, but it's just surface shrinkage. You can seal that yourself with a $20 kit."

"So I don't need you to come out?"

"Nope. Save your money. If it starts widening or leaking, call me."

---

That conversation doesn't sound like how most service businesses operate. No urgency. No upsell. No soft close disguised as concern. Just a contractor telling a customer not to hire him.

But that's exactly the kind of exchange that has defined the reputation of [Attack A Crack](https://www.attackacrack.com/), a family-owned foundation repair and concrete company founded in 2001 by Luc Richard and now serving homeowners across Massachusetts, Connecticut, Rhode Island, New Hampshire, and Maine.

**Where Most Sales Start With Fear**

Foundation issues sit in a uniquely emotional corner of homeownership. They're invisible until they're not. They sound expensive before anyone confirms they are. And once a crack becomes visible in a basement wall or floor, it's hard to unsee. [Research on reinforced concrete](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666496823000298) confirms that genuine structural cracks do compromise durability and shorten a building's lifespan — but most of what homeowners are actually looking at isn't that.

For many homeowners, the first instinct is panic. For many companies, that panic is the opening. Richard built Attack A Crack around the opposite reflex. His partner, [Matt Davis](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mrjmd), who runs the Massachusetts side of the business from Quincy, says the ethos is what drew him into the trade in the first place: an operating principle that most foundation problems are smaller and cheaper to fix than homeowners expect — and that some aren't problems at all. The first move on any call is an honest assessment, often from a single photo sent by text.

**A Specialist, Not a Generalist**

Rather than competing as a general contractor, Attack A Crack operates as a specialist — a focus Richard developed over years of learning how concrete actually behaves in New England's climate. The company works in [foundation crack injection](https://www.attackacrack.com/services/foundation-crack-injection/) using epoxy and polyurethane resins, wall-crack repair, bulkhead sealing, [basement floor crack repair](https://www.attackacrack.com/blog/basement-floor-crack-repair-guide/), and carbon-fiber reinforcement — all backed by a lifetime guarantee.

Nowhere does that approach matter more than with New England's crumbling foundations crisis, where pyrrhotite-contaminated concrete poured decades ago has destroyed thousands of foundations across parts of Massachusetts and Connecticut. Any homeowner in the region who now finds a crack in a basement wall fears the worst. Most calls Attack A Crack gets about it aren't from people who actually have pyrrhotite — that requires a full foundation replacement, which isn't work the company does. They're from people who *fear* they do, and what they usually need is a conversation with someone who can tell the difference between a catastrophe and a shrinkage crack.

**The Contradiction That Builds Trust**

Most people assume expertise leads to more sales. Here, it often leads to fewer. The better you are at diagnosing foundation problems, the more comfortable you are saying: *this isn't one.*

That approach might sound like leaving money on the table. Richard and Davis see it differently. Every homeowner given honest advice without pressure becomes a future customer when a real problem surfaces, a referral source when a neighbor panics, and a review that carries more weight than any advertisement.

The company's core promise is simple: *"When you contact us, you talk to an expert who knows foundations inside and out."* But underneath that, what the two partners have built sells something less tangible — clarity in a moment of uncertainty.

Because when a homeowner asks, "Is my house okay?" they aren't just asking for a service. They're asking for someone they can believe.

Sometimes the most convincing answer a business can give is the one that sounds least like a sale:

"You don't need to hire me."

### Anchor-text principles applied

- **No exact-match spam:** "foundation crack repair" itself (the #1 volume term) is never used as anchor text — it's mentioned in the body but the link goes to the more specific service page via "foundation crack injection."

## Bug report — duplicate hero image in iMessage rich-link previews (May 2, 2026)

### What's happening

When the article URL is shared via iMessage on iOS, the rich-link preview shows the hero photo twice — once inline above the title card and once embedded in the card itself behind the title text. (Doesn't break the link; just looks unprofessional and is the kind of thing readers screenshot.)

Article URL: `https://www.digitaljournal.com/business/this-new-england-foundation-repair-company-built-a-business-talking-people-out-of-repairs/article`

### Root cause

The page emits **two separate `og:image` meta tags**, both pointing at the same hero photo at different WordPress-generated sizes:

```html
<meta property="og:image"        content=".../wp-content/uploads/2026/04/TechTO-9-10.png">          <!-- 1600×900 original -->
<meta property="og:image:width"  content="1600">
<meta property="og:image:height" content="900">
<meta property="og:image:type"   content="image/png">
<meta property="og:image"        content=".../wp-content/uploads/2026/04/TechTO-9-10-1024x576.png"> <!-- 1024×576 auto-scaled duplicate -->
```

The Open Graph spec allows multiple `og:image` tags to declare a *gallery* of distinct images. iOS iMessage honors that — it iterates every `og:image` it finds and renders each one. Because both URLs resolve to the same `TechTO-9-10` photo (just two WordPress size variants of the same upload), the user sees the photo twice instead of once.

The Twitter Card declarations are correct (`twitter:card: summary_large_image` + a single `twitter:image`), so this is exclusively an OG-side emission bug.

### This is sitewide, not specific to my article

I spot-checked 8 other recent `/business/` articles. **8/8 have the same duplicate-`og:image` bug.** Twitter Card is correct on all of them. Two distinct patterns are present:

1. **Older articles (e.g., the AAC piece):** two `og:image` tags pointing at WordPress-generated size variants of the same upload (`X.png` + `X-1024x576.png`).
2. **Newer articles (May 2026):** two `og:image` tags pointing at the *literal identical URL* — pure duplicate emission, no size variant involved. Examples observed:
   - `/business/spirit-airlines-begins-wind-down-cancels-all-flights/article`
   - `/business/exxonmobil-ceo-sees-chance-of-higher-oil-prices-as-earnings-dip/article`
   - `/business/us-fed-official-says-rate-hikes-may-be-needed-if-inflation-surges/article`
   - `/business/trump-says-will-raise-us-tariffs-on-eu-cars-trucks-to-25/article`
   - `/business/gulf-countries-plans-to-bypass-hormuz-still-far-off-experts-warn/article`

Both patterns produce the same iMessage doubling bug. The presence of two distinct patterns suggests one underlying theme/template emitting OG metadata twice — possibly because two SEO plugins are each emitting their own block, or a theme template is rendering the OG block in two places (e.g., once in `<head>` directly and once via a plugin filter).

### Suggested fix

This pattern usually traces back to an SEO plugin (Yoast, RankMath, or a Newspack/theme integration) configured to emit both the original-size featured image AND a WordPress-generated thumbnail variant for the same image. The fix is to emit only ONE `og:image` per unique image:

- **Option 1 (cleanest):** keep only the 1600×900 original `og:image` and remove the duplicate `-1024x576` entry. iOS will use the width/height hints to scale it for the card.
- **Option 2:** keep only the 1024×576 variant (which already matches `twitter:image`) and remove the larger one. Twitter and OG would then declare consistent sources.

Either is fine — what matters is that there's a single `og:image` per unique image so iMessage doesn't render a "gallery."

### How to verify the fix

After deploying, share the article URL in a fresh iMessage thread (or use Facebook's Sharing Debugger at `developers.facebook.com/tools/debug/`, which scrapes OG tags and reports the resolved gallery list). The preview should show the photo exactly once, with the title overlay.

Happy to share the raw diagnostic output if it would help your dev/CMS team.

## Branded social image — design brief

**Source asset:** the same hero photo of Matt + Luc that runs at the top of the Digital Journal article.

**Output:** two crops of the same master file
- 1080×1080 (1:1) for Instagram feed
- 1200×630 (1.91:1) for Facebook + LinkedIn link previews

**Tool recommendation:** Canva (fastest), Figma, Photopea (free, browser), or Photoshop. **Avoid Midjourney/DALL-E for the final composite** — AI image generators cannot reliably render text and will garble "Digital Journal" and the pull quote.

### Composition (top-to-bottom)

1. **Top-left corner, small caps, ~3% of canvas height:**
   `AS FEATURED IN`
   followed immediately below by the Digital Journal wordmark (download from their press kit or screenshot the logo from the article header). White or off-white if the photo background is dark; charcoal if light.

2. **Photo of Matt + Luc** fills the canvas. Apply a subtle linear gradient overlay (40–60% black, fading to transparent) across the bottom third or one side — wherever the pull quote will sit — so the text reads cleanly. Don't darken the faces.

3. **Pull quote in large type, lower-third or left-side:**
   > **"You don't need to hire me."**

   Set in a confident serif (Playfair Display, Source Serif, or anything with a similar editorial feel) or a heavy sans (Inter Bold, Söhne, Helvetica Neue 75 Bold). Quote marks included. White text. Roughly 8–12% of canvas height for the type so it dominates without crowding.

4. **Bottom-right corner:** AAC logo, ~8% of canvas width, single color (white if dark gradient, dark if light gradient).

5. **No URL, no phone, no tagline, no service list.** The image's job is to drive clicks to the article — not to compete with it.

### Alternate pull quotes (if "You don't need to hire me" doesn't land)

- "That's not structural. Save your money."
- "Talking people out of repairs since 2001."
- "Send me a picture."

### If using an AI tool for the background treatment only

If you want to use Photoshop Generative Fill or Adobe Firefly to extend the photo, recolor the background, or add subtle texture *before* you add the text overlays manually, here's a prompt:

> Editorial portrait composite. Two men in foundation repair workwear standing together, natural light, confident relaxed expressions. Soft cinematic color grade — slightly desaturated, warm shadows, neutral highlights. Subtle dark vignette on lower-third for text overlay legibility. Newspaper-feature aesthetic, not stock-photo glossy. Background: New England residential context, slightly defocused. No text, no logos, no graphics — clean image only.

Then bring the result into Canva/Figma/Photoshop and add the wordmark, pull quote, and AAC logo as actual text/vector layers on top.

### What NOT to do

- **Don't reuse Digital Journal's exact composition** — they own the editorial layout. Yours should look like a *companion* asset, not a clone.
- **Don't stack too many elements.** The strongest version of this image has four visual elements total: photo, "as featured in" tag, pull quote, AAC logo. Adding a fifth (URL, phone, tagline) drops engagement — every additional element halves the time someone spends reading the primary one.
- **Don't use a screenshot of the article itself.** Looks like a retweet. Build a native asset.
